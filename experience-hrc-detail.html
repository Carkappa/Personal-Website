The Research Objective

After a hurricane, city planners, public health officials, and emergency responders need to assess physical disorder across large geographic areas. That assessment today is done manually — slow, expensive, and inconsistent.

This research trains computer vision models to detect disorder indicators in street-level imagery, creating a scalable alternative to field surveys that can support data-driven recovery decisions.

Technical Implementation

Data Pipeline

Collected, cleaned, and structured street-level imagery. Designed annotation schema and labeling guidelines ensuring consistency across 5,200+ bounding box labels. Conducted iterative quality review to minimize labeling noise.

Model Training

Fine-tuned YOLO11 on NVIDIA A100 GPU infrastructure. Built reproducible training pipelines covering preprocessing, augmentation, multi-run experiments, and metric-based evaluation. Tracked experiments with WandB.

Interdisciplinary Collaboration

Worked across teams in computer science, urban planning, and public health. Cross-domain collaboration shaped which disorder categories mattered most and aligned model outputs to real recovery use cases.

Key Outcomes

Achieved 80% mAP on NVIDIA A100, establishing a reproducible baseline for iterative improvement.

Labeled ~3,000 images with 5,200+ annotations across 8 disorder categories with consistent quality.

Built end-to-end training pipeline from raw data through evaluation, reproducible for future iterations.

Contributed ML expertise to interdisciplinary team spanning CS, urban planning, and public health.
